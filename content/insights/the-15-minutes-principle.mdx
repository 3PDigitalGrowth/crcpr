In crisis communications advisory, one truth keeps proving itself: the first 15 minutes of your response can define the outcome.

## The 15-minute window

Not that long ago, when a major issue or crisis hit, an organisation had something valuable – time.

There was time before the nightly TV news, the next day's newspaper or the upcoming radio bulletin. The word 'social' meant catching up with your mates at the pub or having a game of social tennis, not a post on Instagram that can be shared thousands of time in a few minutes.

But times have changed, and every year the response window reduces in minutes due to changing technology and digital and social platforms. Now, when a crisis breaks, whether it's a media enquiry about an internal issue, a social media firestorm, or an operational incident that's about to become public, the general principle of crisis experts across the world is that you have approximately 15 minutes to act.

By act, I don't mean develop a full crisis strategy in 15 minutes. That's impossible. Depending on the situation, you likely don't even have many facts within 15 minutes. But the wheels must be put in motion, and if there is anything you can say publicly, like an acknowledgement that you are aware of the situation and are acting, then say it. It sends a powerful message of trust and security.

## Why 15 minutes?

The number isn't arbitrary. In my experience advising companies, governments, and organisations through hundreds of crises, 15 minutes represents the point at which:

- **Media have discovered the story and are developing their angle.** Journalists work fast and are competing with other news outlets to break a story. Within 15 minutes of hearing about an incident, most reporters are already framing their story and seeking comments, and they won't wait for your comment before publishing. They'll often publish with little (or no) information of facts, and correct later (when it can be too late).
- **Social media is establishing a narrative.** On platforms like X, LinkedIn, Instagram, Facebook, Reddit and numerous others, the first posts about an incident set the tone. Once that tone is established, it's exponentially harder to redirect.
- **Internal stakeholders are forming opinions.** Your staff, customers, members, or supporters are all reading the same news and social media feeds. Without clear internal communications, speculation fills the vacuum.

## What to do in the first 15 minutes

The following points may appear impossible in the first few minutes but remember we are usually talking about a situation where something has happened, so you will already have some sort of knowledge – even if very limited - of the issue. It's amazing how much can be achieved if you are prepared and 'crisis ready.'

Keep in mind this is ONLY possible if you have an established and practiced procedure to deal with issues or crises.

1. **Establish the facts.** What is the scope, size and location of the incident or issue? What do you know? What don't you know? What can you confirm? What action is currently being taken to manage incident. Resist the urge to speculate and accept that you only know what you know at this point in time.
2. **Monitor and evaluate public and stakeholder responses.** Is the issue public via mainstream or social media? Is there any stakeholder interaction? What media or public enquiry has been received to date? This information is vital for the right response.
3. **Consult with (already established) outside crisis counsel.** It's difficult to have an objective perspective when you are in the middle of an issue, and right now that perspective is vital. What you do now sets the course, so you need someone on the outside who can speak with calmness, objectivity and experience in these matters.
4. **Ask – "Is this a containable issue or emerging crisis?"** Why is this important? Because if you get this wrong your entire response strategy won't be as effective and could even make things worse. A containable issue and a likely escalating crisis require different approaches, strategies and personnel.
5. **Activate your key team or crisis team.** Depending on your evaluation of the issue, you'll know who to involve immediately. If you don't have a defined response or crisis team with clear roles, you've already lost time. This is why crisis communications planning is not optional.
6. **Prepare holding statements.** You won't be able to provide all the answers immediately, but will need to demonstrate that you are aware of the issue, taking it seriously and have commenced dealing with it. You'll also need to identify, address and relate to any emotions stakeholders are feeling, and provide an assurance of continued communication.
7. **Ensure spokespeople are prepared.** They must know the key messages. They must also understand the emotions stakeholders are feeling so they can connect with them. If they are not media trained there will likely be problems.
8. **Control internal communications.** Your staff and other key stakeholders should hear from you before they hear from the media.

## The cost of delay

Every minute beyond that 15-minute window increases the likelihood that the initial narrative will be shaped by others: journalists, competitors, social media commentators, or disgruntled stakeholders.

My friend and host of our crisis masterclass, Melissa Agnes - CEO of the Crisis Ready Institute who has consulted to international organisations like NASA and the Pentagon - puts it this way:

> "The Crisis Response Penalty is when organisations fail to respond effectively in the right timeline. The longer you wait, the more you lose ownership of the narrative, the more trust erodes, and the greater the material impact becomes."

You can't control THE narrative because you can't control what others say and do. But you can control YOUR narrative and massaging. The organisations that navigate crises successfully are those who are prepared in advance, connect with their stakeholders, respond well, and communicate regularly.
