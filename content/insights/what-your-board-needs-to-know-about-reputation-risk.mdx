Reputation risk appears on most corporate risk registers. In most organisations, it sits somewhere between operational risk and strategic risk. It is rarely interrogated with the same rigour as financial risk, compliance risk, or safety risk.

This is a governance gap. And it is most visible at board level.

## Why boards underestimate reputation risk

Reputation risk is difficult to quantify. You cannot put it on a balance sheet. Its probability is hard to calculate. And because reputation damage often unfolds through the media or social media (channels that boards are not always monitoring closely), the signals can be easy to miss.

## The questions every board should be asking

1. **Do we have a tested crisis communications plan?** "Tested" means exercised under simulated pressure, not a document that was approved and filed.

2. **Who is our designated spokesperson for a crisis?** And have they received recent media training?

3. **What are our top three reputation risks right now?** Not in general. Specifically. Named, described, and assessed.

4. **How would we know if a reputation issue was emerging?** What monitoring systems are in place?

5. **Have we engaged external communications counsel before we needed them?** Relationships built before a crisis are worth substantially more than cold introductions during one.

## The governance imperative

Directors have both a legal and ethical obligation to ensure their organisation is prepared for foreseeable risks. Reputational crises are foreseeable. The fact that their exact form cannot be predicted does not remove the obligation to prepare.
