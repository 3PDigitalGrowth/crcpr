In crisis communications advisory, one truth keeps proving itself: the first 15 minutes of your response define the outcome of the entire crisis.

## The 15-minute window

When a crisis breaks, whether it's a media enquiry about an internal issue, a social media firestorm, or an operational incident that's about to become public, you have approximately 15 minutes to establish control of the narrative.

After that window closes, you're no longer shaping the story. You're responding to it.

## Why 15 minutes?

The number isn't arbitrary. In my experience advising companies, governments, and organisations through hundreds of crises, 15 minutes represents the point at which:

- **Media have committed to their angle.** Journalists work fast. Within 15 minutes of hearing about an incident, most reporters have already framed their story and begun seeking confirmation quotes.
- **Social media has established the narrative.** On platforms like X, LinkedIn, and Facebook, the first posts about an incident set the tone. Once that tone is established, it's exponentially harder to redirect.
- **Internal stakeholders have formed opinions.** Your board, your staff, and your members are all reading the same news and social media feeds. Without clear internal communications, speculation fills the vacuum.

## What to do in the first 15 minutes

1. **Activate your crisis team.** If you don't have a defined crisis team with clear roles, you've already lost time. This is why crisis communications planning is not optional.
2. **Establish the facts.** What do you know? What don't you know? What can you confirm? Resist the urge to speculate.
3. **Prepare a holding statement.** You don't need all the answers immediately. You need to demonstrate that you're aware, you're taking it seriously, and you'll provide more information as it becomes available.
4. **Identify your spokesperson.** Who will speak publicly? Are they prepared? Have they been media trained?
5. **Control internal communications.** Your staff should hear from you before they hear from the media.

## The cost of delay

Every minute beyond that 15-minute window increases the likelihood that the narrative will be shaped by others: journalists, competitors, social media commentators, or disgruntled stakeholders.

The organisations that navigate crises successfully are not the ones that never face them. They're the ones that respond first, respond well, and maintain control of the message throughout.
