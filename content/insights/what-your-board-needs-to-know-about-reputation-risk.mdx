Reputation risk appears on most corporate risk registers, yet in most organisations, it is rarely considered with the same rigour as financial risk, compliance risk, or safety risk. However when you consider that all risks have an element of reputation risk, and that reputation risk is linked to the erosion of trust that brings down CEO and sometimes entire organisations, a dismissive or ignorant attitude to reputational vulnerability is pure folly.

This is a governance gap, and it is most visible at board level. I have found that boards often leave crisis preparedness and reputational vulnerabilities to the executive leadership, and think it's not their role. Yet the board must be involved. It's not an option.

## Why boards underestimate reputation risk

Maybe they believe that reputation risk is difficult to quantify (it's actually not) because it can't be added to a balance sheet and may be difficult to calculate. Maybe because reputation damage often unfolds through the media or social media (channels that boards are not always monitoring closely), the signals can be easy to miss.

## Why boards must be involved in reputation risk

- Directors are ultimately responsible for the health and future of the organisation, and any organisation that is not mitigating vulnerabilities that can lead to reputation damage is unhealthy.
- Directors can be held personally liable for breaches of some laws, negligence, and failures in some areas including work health and safety (source – Australian Institute of Company Directors).
- Directors must act with care and diligence.
- The board will become involved in issues or crises concerning the CEO or senior executive leadership by necessity.
- A board representative is the most appropriate person to be involved in some stakeholder communication and as a media spokesperson when issues arise that involve the CEO or governance.

## The questions every board should be asking

- **Is our organisation prepared to handle a crisis?**
- **Do we have a tested crisis communications plan?** "Tested" means exercised under simulated pressure, not a document that was approved and filed.
- **Who is our designated board spokesperson for a crisis?** And have they received recent media training?
- **In what areas are we vulnerable to reputation risk now?** And what are we doing about it?
- **How would we know if a reputation issue was emerging?** What monitoring systems are in place?
- **Have we engaged external crisis communications counsel before we needed them?** Relationships built before a crisis are worth substantially more than cold introductions during one.

## The governance imperative

Directors have both a legal and ethical obligation to ensure their organisation is prepared for foreseeable risks. Reputational crises are foreseeable. The fact that their exact form cannot be predicted does not remove the obligation to prepare.
