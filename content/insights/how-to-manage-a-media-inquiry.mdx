A journalist has just called your office. Your assistant has put them on hold. You have 30 seconds to decide how to respond.

Most executives, even experienced ones, make at least one of three mistakes in this moment: they answer unprepared, they say "no comment," or they promise to call back and then delay too long. Each of these erodes your position.

Here is what to do instead.

## Step 1: Buy yourself time legitimately

You are entitled to a call-back window. Say: "Thank you for your call. [Name] is currently in a commitment but will call you back within the hour. Can I take your deadline?" This is not evasion. It is professional practice. Journalists expect it.

A callback window gives you time to: understand what the story is, establish the facts, prepare your key messages, and brief your spokesperson.

## Step 2: Establish the story before you respond

Before any spokesperson speaks to media, answer four questions:
- What does the journalist already know?
- What is the likely angle of the story?
- What are the facts we can confirm?
- What is our key message, stated in one sentence?

If you don't know the story, ask. Call the journalist back and say: "I want to make sure I'm giving you the most relevant information. Can you tell me what the story is about?" Most will tell you.

## Step 3: Prepare three messages, not ten

Media interviews are won and lost on message discipline. Your spokesperson needs three clear, concise, evidence-backed messages, not a list of everything the organisation wants to say.

## Step 4: Decide who speaks

Not every executive is the right spokesperson for every situation. The most credible spokesperson is the one who knows the subject matter, has been media trained, and has authority in the eyes of the audience.

## Step 5: Never say "no comment"

"No comment" is never a neutral position. It is interpreted as confirmation that there is something to hide. If you cannot answer a specific question, say why and return to your prepared message.
